Proof Safety Rule

Warm-intro proof stays strong when trust rises but names, screenshots, pricing, and hidden mechanics stay controlled.

The right proof-safe layer answers five things fast: what the result was, whether the buyer can stay anonymous, whether the partner can be named, what remains off-limits, and where the approved proof should be reused next.

Safe Formats

Most warm-intro proof can fit one of these smaller, cleaner formats.

Anonymous Result Line

One short line about the account type, the bottleneck, and the shift achieved, without naming the buyer.

Brandless Before-After

A stripped summary of what changed, without screenshots, logos, or identifiable page references.

Partner Quote

One line from the partner about what became easier, faster, or cleaner, without exposing hidden workflow or economics.

Private Approval Note

A non-public summary that helps an internal approver say yes without turning into public marketing proof.

Redacted Snippet

A partially masked result detail that preserves trust without exposing sensitive routes, numbers, or names.

Off-Limits Reminder

One explicit reminder about what cannot be forwarded keeps later reuse safer than vague assumptions.

Do Not Reuse

These details usually create trust damage faster than they create trust.

Brand Names By Default

Do not assume buyer names, partner names, or internal brands are available for reuse just because the work closed cleanly.

Screenshots Without Clearance

Screenshots reveal more than expected: brand, context, metrics, and hidden working structure.

Hidden Economics

Margins, pricing logic, payout structure, and markup should stay private unless explicitly cleared.

Internal Mechanics

Delivery workflow, SOP detail, tooling, and behind-the-scenes process usually are not needed in buyer-facing proof.

Broad Case-Study Dumping

The goal is one safe proof line that helps the next move, not a giant proof folder that raises exposure risk.

Implied Naming Rights

Approval of the current step or a positive reply does not equal permission to name people publicly.
